Knowledge Representation

Knowledge Representation (KR) is a field in artificial intelligence concerned with how to formally encode information about the world so that a computer system can reason, infer, and act upon it. It bridges human understanding and machine processing.

1. Goals of KR

  • Enable automated reasoning and inference
  • Facilitate communication between agents and systems
  • Support decision-making and problem-solving
  • Model real-world entities and relationships

2. Representation Paradigms

  • Logic-based: Propositional and predicate logic
  • Semantic networks: Graphs of concepts and relations
  • Frames: Structured templates with slots and values
  • Ontologies: Formal domain models with reasoning
  • Rules: IFâ€“THEN constructs for inference

3. Logical Foundations

  • First-order logic (FOL)
  • Horn clauses and resolution
  • Non-monotonic and modal logic
  • Temporal and fuzzy logic extensions

4. Inference Mechanisms

  • Deductive reasoning (truth-preserving)
  • Abductive reasoning (hypothesis generation)
  • Inductive reasoning (generalization from examples)
  • Forward and backward chaining

5. KR Languages

  • Prolog (logic programming)
  • OWL (ontology-based reasoning)
  • CLIPS and Jess (rule-based systems)
  • KIF (Knowledge Interchange Format)

6. Applications

  • Expert systems and decision support
  • Natural language understanding
  • Semantic search and intelligent agents
  • Robotics and autonomous systems
  • Medical diagnosis and legal reasoning

7. Challenges

  • Scalability and expressiveness trade-offs
  • Ambiguity and incomplete knowledge
  • Integration across heterogeneous sources
  • Balancing symbolic and statistical approaches

8. Future Directions

  • Hybrid KR with machine learning
  • Commonsense reasoning and cognitive architectures
  • Explainable AI through symbolic models
  • KR for multi-agent and distributed systems
